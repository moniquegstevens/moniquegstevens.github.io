Process Design

The onboarding system I built because I couldn't just let it stay broken

When I joined my current company, I was placed on orientation calls to answer first day questions. Pretty quickly I started noticing a pattern: new hires were showing up on day one without laptops, login credentials, or a clear point of contact. It was stressful for them and honestly unnecessary. So I went around and talked to every team involved, mapped every gap, and built a system that connected all six of them: IT, the TA team, HR Solutions Center, L&D, hiring managers, and HRIS. I also own all requisition creation for the company, meaning every hire that enters the pipeline starts with me.

I built a master Excel tracker using Power Query to auto-refresh directly from Dayforce daily, pulling in new hire data without manual entry. VLOOKUPs pulled in IT equipment tracking numbers and XLOOKUPs connected live background check statuses. I am the only person who can edit the sheet. Every piece of data in it came through me. Each team sends me their reports and I enter it myself. The expectation is simple: I need everything in my hands by 12pm Thursday. If I do not have it, I have questions.

The dashboard is maintained every afternoon with the latest updates. We operate in an Agile environment which means things change constantly. If a candidate rescinded, pushed their start date, or dropped, I was informed immediately and the sheet was updated in real time. Nothing sat. Once updated, I pushed a message to the Teams group chat and required a thumbs up from each team to confirm receipt. No ambiguity, no "I didn't see it." Everyone was accountable.

The Thursday 2pm sync is where everything gets confirmed and anything still outstanding gets resolved on that same call. After the call, I send a final summary to the group: how many starts on Monday, how many pushes, how many rescind. Clean, confirmed, closed. Thursday is the hard cutoff because it is the last window to act. On rare occasions a Friday expedite happens but that is a special situation, not the norm. Once Thursday closes, the week is done.

Budget, headcount, HRBPs, and Finance have read-only access so leadership always has full visibility. But no one touches the data but me. The sheet is mine. The process is mine. The 99% success rate reflects that.

99% day-one success rate 6 teams coordinated in one system Power Query auto-refresh from Dayforce Teams confirmation required from every team Friday expedite as rare exception only

Retention Strategy

The exit interview program I built because I kept seeing the same names twice

I started noticing something while creating requisitions for the sales team. I would open a role, it would get filled, I would see the new hire's name come through my master sheet and chat with them at orientation, and then a few weeks later I would be signing their resignation form. Then I would reopen that same req and enter them as the vacating employee. Same name, same role, over and over. It bothered me.

The team was sitting at around 75% of their target headcount and nobody had a clear picture of why people were leaving so quickly. Nobody was doing exit interviews either, so I raised my hand and volunteered to run them. Within the first call I found multiple root causes across the pre-onboarding experience: gaps in the handoff between recruiting and what the role actually looked like on day one, outdated onboarding materials, and a lack of manager visibility during those critical first weeks. I documented everything including the overall tone and energy of each conversation, and brought a full summary to the VP of HR and Director of Recruiting. They loved it. The program now directly shapes the company's sales retention strategy and the issues that once affected the majority of new hires have become rare.

25% headcount gap identified on entry 50%+ of leavers shared common root causes Program built from scratch Presented directly to VP of HR Now informs company retention strategy

Requisition Governance

The gatekeeper for every hire that enters the pipeline

Every requisition at my company flows through me before a recruiter ever sees it. When a manager submits a request ticket in Dayforce, I verify the pay range with the HRBP, confirm union status, validate that the location ties to the correct title, and if the title is new I work with HRIS to get it added. Then I consult our approval matrix to identify the manager's approval path and the recruiter tied to it, build the req, and route it. I provide status updates to hiring managers by monitoring the workflow admin directly in Dayforce. On a typical day I handle around 10 individual Dayforce req inquiry tickets.

After the merger I closed out and routed approximately 600 requisition requests in a single month. I also manage mass req workflows for managers who submit bulk requests via the Mass Req Tracker, a shared Excel workbook I built where each of the five managers I support owns their own tab. They add their requests, ping me in Teams when a new batch is ready, and I create each req and drop the req number back into their tab. I run 2 to 3 batches per month depending on project demands, with each batch covering up to 60 to 70 reqs across all managers.

Post-merger I also took ownership of the Committee Review process for non-mission-critical roles. I maintain a list of mission-critical titles that route through the standard approval path. Everything else goes onto a separate Excel tracker reviewed by Finance every two weeks for headcount and budget approval. In Dayforce I update each ticket status to "Committee" so managers always know where their request stands. Once a decision is made I update the ticket and an automated notification goes to the recruiter and hiring manager with either the req number or a denial notice.

650+ reqs routed in one month post-merger Up to 70 mass reqs per batch, 2 to 3 times a month 5 managers supported via Mass Req Tracker ~10 Dayforce inquiry tickets handled daily Mission-critical and committee review governance
